First Aid

First aid is the immediate care a sick or injured person gets. In some cases, it may be the only care a person needs. In others, first aid is a way to prevent a person's condition from worsening and keep them alive until paramedics arrive or they are taken to the hospital.

The best way to prepare for these events is to get official first-aid training. In the meantime, there are some basic life-saving steps you can learn. This article goes over the first aid steps to follow in different situations and how to tell if more care is needed.

The Basics

First aid is about assessing the situation and providing immediate care. Begin by ensuring the area is safe for you and the injured person, then assess the situation. Call emergency services if necessary. For minor injuries, such as cuts or bruises, clean the wound and apply a bandage. For more serious conditions like fractures or unconsciousness, focus on stabilizing the person until professional help arrives. Always stay calm and provide reassurance to the injured person.

Stopped Heart

In the event of cardiac arrest, immediate action is crucial. First, check the person's responsiveness by shouting their name and gently shaking them. If there’s no response and they’re not breathing, start CPR. Place the heel of your hand on the center of their chest and push hard and fast, aiming for 100-120 compressions per minute. If trained, give two rescue breaths after every 30 compressions. If an AED is available, turn it on and follow the instructions. Continue CPR until emergency responders arrive.

Bleeding

To control bleeding, start by applying firm, direct pressure to the wound with a clean cloth or bandage. Elevating the injured limb above the heart can also help reduce bleeding. Once the bleeding slows, wrap a bandage tightly around the wound to maintain pressure. For severe bleeding, continue applying pressure and seek immediate medical help if the bleeding doesn't stop within 10 minutes or if the wound is deep or large.

Choking

If someone is choking, ask if they can speak or cough. If they cannot, perform the Heimlich maneuver by standing behind the person and wrapping your arms around their waist. Make a fist and place it just above their navel, then thrust inward and upward sharply to dislodge the object. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious, in which case CPR should be started immediately. For babies, gentle back blows and chest thrusts are more appropriate.

Burns

Burns can be caused by heat, chemicals, or electricity. For minor burns, cool the burn by running it under cold water for at least 10 minutes, then cover it with a clean, non-stick bandage. Avoid applying ice directly or using ointments, which can cause further damage. For severe burns, do not remove any clothing stuck to the burn, and seek medical attention immediately. Electrical burns require medical help, even if they appear minor.

Blisters

Blisters form to protect the underlying skin, so it’s generally best to leave them intact. Clean the area with mild soap and water, and cover the blister with a loose bandage or moleskin pad. If the blister is large or painful, you can drain it using a sterilized needle. Pierce the edge of the blister, let the fluid drain, but leave the overlying skin intact to protect the area. Always keep the area clean and covered to avoid infection.

Broken Bone/Fracture

For suspected broken bones, keep the injured area still and avoid moving the person unless absolutely necessary. If you need to move them, use a splint to immobilize the injured limb. You can create a splint using something rigid, like a board or rolled-up newspaper, securing it with strips of cloth. Apply ice to reduce swelling, and seek immediate medical help. Avoid giving the injured person food or drink in case surgery is needed.

Sprains

Sprains occur when ligaments are stretched or torn. To treat a sprain, remember the R.I.C.E. method: Rest the injured area, Ice it to reduce swelling (apply for 20 minutes every hour), Compress with an elastic bandage, and Elevate the limb above the heart to minimize swelling. Over-the-counter pain relievers can help reduce discomfort. Seek medical attention if you suspect a more serious injury or if swelling and pain persist.

Nosebleeds

To stop a nosebleed, have the person sit up straight and lean slightly forward. Pinch the soft part of their nose, just below the bridge, and apply pressure for about 10-15 minutes. Avoid tilting the head back, as this can cause blood to run down the throat. If the bleeding persists for more than 20 minutes, or if the nosebleed is the result of a serious injury, seek medical help.

Frostbite

Frostbite occurs when skin and tissue freeze due to prolonged exposure to cold. The affected area may become numb, white, or hard. To treat frostbite, move the person to a warmer environment and gently rewarm the affected areas by soaking them in warm (not hot) water. Avoid using direct heat like fires or heaters, which can cause burns. Do not rub the skin, as this can cause further tissue damage. Seek medical attention for deep or severe frostbite.

Bee Sting

For a bee sting, remove the stinger as quickly as possible by scraping it with a flat object (like a credit card). Wash the area with soap and water, then apply a cold compress to reduce swelling. Over-the-counter pain relievers and antihistamines can help alleviate pain and itching. If the person shows signs of an allergic reaction, such as difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, or dizziness, administer an epinephrine injection if available and seek emergency medical help immediately.

First Aid Kit List

A well-stocked first aid kit is essential. Items to include are bandages of various sizes, sterile gauze pads, adhesive tape, antiseptic wipes, tweezers, scissors, a thermometer, disposable gloves, a first aid manual, pain relievers like aspirin or ibuprofen, an instant cold pack, and any personal medications (such as an EpiPen for allergic reactions). Keep the kit in a cool, dry place and check its contents regularly to ensure nothing is expired or missing.
